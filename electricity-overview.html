🔗 What you'll explore

Add 1, 2, or 3 identical bulbs in series. Watch the current stay the same everywhere, but each bulb get dimmer as you add more.

👀 What to observe

  • The current readings are identical at every point in the loop.
  • Adding a bulb → total resistance ↑ → current ↓ → every bulb dims.
  • The voltage across each identical bulb is Vbattery ÷ N.

👀 What to observe

  • Voltage across every working branch = battery voltage (not split).
  • Adding a branch leaves each bulb's brightness unchanged, but the total current from the battery rises.
  • Blow a bulb → only that branch dies; the rest keep glowing.

📈 What you'll explore

Sweep voltage and resistance separately. The current bar reacts instantly, and the I-vs-V graph shows the straight-line relationship at constant R.

👀 What to observe

  • Double the voltage at constant R → current doubles. I ∝ V.
  • Double the resistance at constant V → current halves. I ∝ 1/R.
  • The graph line is always straight through the origin — that's Ohm's Law.

📖 The two rules

  • Ammeter — measures current. Has near-zero resistance, so it must go in series with the component (in the main loop).
  • Voltmeter — measures potential difference. Has very high resistance, so it must go in parallel, across the two ends of the component.

Why it matters

  • Ammeter in parallel = a short circuit. Huge current flows and the ammeter (or wire) burns out.
  • Voltmeter in series = it chokes the circuit; the bulb stays dark and the voltmeter just reads the battery voltage.

💧 What you'll explore

The battery is a water tower. Each drop carries a fixed amount of energy when it leaves the top. Drop colour = energy each drop still has (= PD). Drop count per second = current. At a junction, current splits — but energy per drop does NOT.

👀 What to watch

  • At the same height in any branch, the drops are the same colour — same PD per charge regardless of branch.
  • In series: each drop dims a little at every waterwheel it passes (energy splits across the wheels).
  • In parallel: each branch handles its own drops — a wheel only sees the drops in its branch, but each of those drops gives up its full energy in that wheel.
  • Drop density (how many per second) = current. Watch the trunk pipe before the split — it's denser than each branch after the split.
